# Training Philosophy

This document defines the underlying training principles used by the AI coach.

## Source

This philosophy is based on:

- Joe Friel – The Cyclist’s Training Bible
- Joe Friel – Fast After 50
- Internal training materials and presentations

---

## 1. Core Principles

### Specificity

Training should become more specific as the target event approaches.

- Early phases → general fitness
- Later phases → event-specific demands

---

### Progressive Overload

Fitness improves through gradual increases in training stress:

- increase volume and/or intensity over time
- followed by recovery periods to allow adaptation

---

### Consistency

- long-term consistency is more important than short-term intensity
- avoid large fluctuations in training load

---

### Individualization

Training must consider:

- age
- recovery ability
- limiter profile
- available training time

---

## 2. Periodization

### Macrocycle

- full season structure
- aligned with key events (A races or goals)

---

### Mesocycles

Typical structure:

- 3 weeks progressive load
- 1 week recovery

For older athletes (50+):

- 2 weeks load
- 1 week recovery (recommended)

---

### External Planning Systems (IMPORTANT)

- If a structured training plan is provided (e.g. from intervals.icu):
  - use the defined phases (Base, Build, Peak, Transition)
  - use the planned weekly load (TSS) as primary guidance

- These external plans:
  - define the intended periodization
  - should be respected and followed whenever available

- However:
  - they are NOT mandatory
  - if missing, inconsistent, or clearly inappropriate:
    → apply standard Friel-based periodization instead

- The AI coach operates primarily on the microcycle (weekly level),
  while mesocycles are typically defined externally.

---

### Microcycle (Weekly Structure)

Each week should include:

- 1 key VO2max session
- 1 key threshold session
- 1 long aerobic ride
- remaining sessions: endurance or recovery

---

## 3. Training Phases

### Base Phase

Focus:

- aerobic endurance
- efficiency
- basic strength

Typical training:

- long Z2 rides
- low intensity volume
- limited high intensity

---

### Build Phase

Focus:

- increasing FTP
- developing VO2max
- muscular endurance

Typical training:

- VO2max intervals
- threshold sessions
- longer sustained efforts

---

### Peak Phase

Focus:

- race-specific performance
- sharpening intensity
- reduced volume

Typical training:

- high intensity, lower volume
- race simulation
- freshness prioritization

---

### Transition Phase

Focus:

- recovery
- mental reset
- maintaining basic fitness

---

## 4. Intensity Distribution

### 80/20 Principle

- ~80% low intensity (Z1–Z2)
- ~20% moderate to high intensity

Purpose:

- maximize adaptation
- minimize fatigue accumulation

---

### Training Distribution Models

Common patterns:

- Base → predominantly low intensity
- Pyramidal → majority low, some threshold, little high intensity
- Polarized → high volume low + some high intensity, minimal threshold
- Threshold-heavy → larger proportion in Z3–Z4

The appropriate distribution depends on:

- training phase
- athlete limiter
- fatigue level

---

## 5. Key Performance Factors

### Aerobic Endurance

- foundation of all performance
- developed through long, steady rides

---

### Threshold (FTP)

- ability to sustain high power over time
- critical for long climbs

---

### VO2max

- maximal aerobic capacity
- supports improvements in FTP

Important for athletes 50+:

- must be trained year-round to slow decline

---

### Aerobic Durability

- ability to maintain power over long durations
- assessed via decoupling

---

### Fueling

- directly impacts endurance and durability
- insufficient fueling can mimic fitness limitations

---

## 6. Limiter-Based Training

Training should prioritize the primary limiter:

- VO2max limiter → increase high-intensity work
- threshold limiter → increase sustained efforts
- durability limiter → increase long rides and fueling
- fueling limiter → improve carbohydrate strategy

---

## 7. Training Load (TSS Concept)

Training load combines:

- duration
- intensity

Reference:

- 1 hour at FTP ≈ 100 TSS

Usage:

- manage weekly load
- track fitness (CTL)
- track fatigue (ATL)
- evaluate form (CTL - ATL)

---

## 8. Recovery and Adaptation

Adaptation occurs during recovery, not during training.

Indicators of recovery:

- HRV
- resting heart rate
- subjective fatigue

Guidelines:

- include regular recovery weeks
- reduce load when fatigue is high
- prioritize sleep and nutrition

---

## 9. Age-Specific Considerations (50+)

- reduced recovery capacity
- higher importance of intensity maintenance
- VO2max training is mandatory year-round
- shorter build cycles recommended

---

## 10. Key Principle

Training decisions must balance:

- stress (training load)
- recovery (adaptation capacity)

The goal is to apply enough stress to stimulate adaptation  
without exceeding the athlete’s ability to recover.

---

## 11. Practical Application

All training plans should:

- follow the phase structure
- respect fatigue and recovery
- include key sessions each week
- align with the athlete’s goal (long climbs, FTP)

Training is not static:

- it must be continuously adjusted based on feedback and data
